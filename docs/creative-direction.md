# Creative Direction

## One-Line Pitch

A Steam Deck-first cozy systems narrative game about starting a small nursery in a strange, living country, learning the needs of plants and people, and becoming part of a place through patience, care, and local knowledge.

## Core Identity

This is not "Stardew but plants." The nursery is the playable ritual, but the deeper subject is learning how to live in relation with the world.

The game should make the player feel:

- The quiet pride of helping something survive.
- The warmth of being known by a town.
- The humility of adapting to conditions you do not control.
- The delight of discovering local weirdness.
- The tension between humane care and systems that reward extraction, speed, and disconnection.

The game is gentle and hopeful, but not naive. It should acknowledge coldness, scarcity, loneliness, bureaucracy, economic pressure, and technology pulling people apart. The answer is not a clean good/evil split. The answer is attention, reciprocity, and making better choices inside imperfect incentives.

## Format

- Engine: Godot 4.x.
- Platform target: Steam Deck first.
- Mode: single-player.
- Camera: 2D or 2.5D, likely isometric or diorama-like.
- Structure: campaign/sandbox hybrid.
- World: fictional country with 5-6 regions.
- Initial scope: build one starter region first.

## Player Character

The player should have meaningful character customization:

- Name.
- Pronouns.
- Appearance.
- Nursery name.
- Maybe a few background-flavor choices.

The protagonist can still have a light fixed premise: they are starting over after leaving a life that made them feel alienated from themselves. Keep this broad enough for self-insert play.

The protagonist should be mostly quiet in moment-to-moment dialogue, but not empty. Their choices, recommendations, nursery style, and relationships express who they are.

Reference shape: Stardew Valley's "left the old life behind" premise, but not the same inheritance story.

## Starting Situation

The player begins with a very small roadside nursery or farm stand, not a full garden center.

This keeps the opening understandable:

- A table.
- A few crates.
- A seed tray.
- A ledger.
- A hand-painted sign.
- A handful of customers.

The nursery can grow into a richer space over time: benches, greenhouse, shade house, propagation room, display gardens, checkout shed, community board, and regional specialties.

## World Structure

The country should be invented, but grounded. It can have magical plants, unusual customs, and regional folklore, but it should feel like a place with history, weather, institutions, trade, class, and ecology.

Plan for 5-6 regions, but build only one first.

### Starter Region

The first region should be welcoming and readable for new players.

Design goals:

- Forgiving climate.
- Familiar plant analogues.
- Clear seasonal changes.
- Friendly early characters.
- Gentle magical realism.
- Strong tutorial fit.

This region should not be bland. It needs local texture, customs, arguments, beloved crops, weather quirks, and plants that make it feel distinct.

### Hot/Extreme Region

Plan a region where heat is the dominant constraint.

This could be a modified steppe/desert/highland region, or a separate region later.

Design goals:

- Extreme heat.
- Water scarcity.
- Shade and irrigation strategy.
- Drought-adapted plants.
- Heat-loving magical crops.
- Market pressure around survival, comfort, and water politics.

The player should need to adapt. Strategies that work in the starter region should not transfer cleanly.

### Cold/Hard-Mode Region

Plan a late or hard-mode region with a very cold climate and short growing season.

Design goals:

- Short growing window.
- Frost risk.
- Greenhouse and season-extension systems.
- Hardy perennials and storage crops.
- High value on timing, planning, and microclimates.
- Emotional tone of endurance, patience, and beauty under constraint.

This should be one of the clearest tests of mastery. The player succeeds by understanding the place, not by brute-forcing the same crop everywhere.

### Other Possible Regions

- Foggy coast: salt wind, fungi, glasshouses, medicinal herbs.
- Volcanic upland: mineral soil, rare bulbs, heat pockets, old shrines.
- Marsh/fen: bog gardens, insects, flood cycles, old folk rituals.
- Dense old-city region: rooftop gardens, pollution, class pressure, tiny plots, beauty in constrained spaces.

## Market-Reading Pillar

Understanding the local market should be a core path to success.

A crop analogue that performs well in Region A, Year 1 should not be a universal solution. Demand should shift by:

- Region.
- Season.
- Weather.
- Local events.
- Customer demographics.
- Economic conditions.
- Cultural taste.
- Previous player choices.
- Supply gluts or shortages.
- Rumors, fashion, and social pressure.

The player should be able to make informed predictions, but never with total certainty.

Information should come through the world, not just a spreadsheet:

- Customer dialogue.
- Supplier chatter.
- Local newspaper or radio.
- Community board notes.
- Festival requests.
- Neighbor gossip.
- Field observations.
- Weather signs.
- Plant performance.
- What customers browse but do not buy.
- What sells out at competitors or markets.

The goal is not to punish the player for guessing wrong. The goal is to make paying attention feel powerful.

## Writing Pillars

Writing is load-bearing. It is not flavor text pasted on top of a sim.

The writing should be:

- Warm.
- Funny when it can be.
- Specific.
- Patient.
- Observant.
- Hopeful without lying.
- Comfortable with moral gray areas.

Avoid:

- Generic cozy-game wholesomeness.
- Corny speeches about nature healing everything.
- Clean villains whose only role is to be wrong.
- Lore dumps that explain magic too neatly.
- Player flattery.

The better line is: nature does not fix you, but it gives you a way to practice being alive differently.

## Character Design

Recurring characters should be friendly, memorable, and under pressure.

Each important character should have:

- A desire.
- A contradiction.
- A relationship to plants.
- A pressure from the wider world.
- A way they change over seasons.

Examples:

- A city planner pushing water restrictions who is exhausted, correct about scarcity, bad at talking to people, and secretly trying to keep their mother's rose alive.
- A new homeowner terrified of killing everything because they treat every mistake as proof they are not cut out for care.
- A chef who starts by wanting perfect herbs and slowly becomes interested in soil, seasonality, and patience.
- A collector who seems silly until the player realizes their rare plants are a way of surviving loneliness.
- A child who names every plant and notices things adults miss.
- A burned-out landscaper who still recognizes beauty but no longer trusts that beauty can matter.

## Lessons From Reference Games

### Undertale

- Kindness and violence are mechanical, not just thematic.
- Characters are funny, specific, wounded, and unforgettable.
- Replay value comes from changed relationships and changed meaning.

Garden Nursery takeaway: care should alter systems and relationships. The player should feel that attention leaves traces.

### Wanderstop

- The cozy ritual works because it is emotionally necessary, not just decorative.
- A gentle activity can reveal internal struggle without becoming grim.

Garden Nursery takeaway: nursery work should be a playable form of stillness, not chores for their own sake.

### Suzerain

- People are shaped by incentives, institutions, fear, pride, and scarcity.
- Good intentions can still cause harm.
- Systems should create gray-area choices.

Garden Nursery takeaway: market, regulation, suppliers, water, landlords, technology, and class should pressure characters without flattening them into villains.

### Morrowind

- A world feels real when it is strange, regional, old, and not over-explained.
- Local customs and factions make replay feel like discovery.

Garden Nursery takeaway: magical plants and regional customs should feel matter-of-fact and locally embedded.

## Magical Realism

Use magical realism, not fantasy quest logic.

Magic should reveal relationships, histories, care, and contradictions. It should not become a generic power system.

Plant ideas:

- A vine that repeats the last kind thing said near it, imperfectly.
- A tomato analogue that glows at dusk under specific growing conditions.
- A moon herb that wilts near lies and recovers after apology.
- A root vegetable that only grows in neglected corners.
- A pollinator flower that changes color near unresolved arguments.
- A crop that mimics the player's voice or rhythm badly enough to be funny and unsettling.

Keep normal, familiar crops or close analogues too. The magic lands better when it grows beside ordinary herbs, greens, flowers, and vegetables.

## Art Direction

Target a cozy botanical illustration style rather than default pixel art.

Direction:

- 2D or 2.5D.
- Isometric/diorama nursery spaces.
- Hand-painted, gouache, colored-pencil, or storybook botanical feel.
- Strong plant silhouettes.
- Readable UI.
- Seasonal lighting.
- Plant tags, seed packets, handwritten notes, market boards, ledgers, and nursery signs as UI inspiration.

Avoid:

- Beige soup.
- Generic fantasy farm visuals.
- Overly slick mobile-game UI.
- Tiny text or hover-only interactions.

## Steam Deck Constraints

Design for Steam Deck from the start.

Baseline:

- 1280x800 target layout.
- Controller-first interaction.
- Large readable text.
- No required mouse precision.
- No hover-only information.
- Fast suspend/resume friendliness.
- Stable performance for a 2D game.
- Satisfying 10-20 minute play sessions.

The UI should feel good with D-pad/stick navigation and face buttons before it is optimized for keyboard/mouse.

## Replay Value

Replay should come from regional adaptation and identity, not random noise alone.

Levers:

- Starting region.
- Nursery specialization.
- Customer relationships.
- Market shifts.
- Plant discovery and hybrids.
- Local events.
- Economic pressures.
- Different ways to be a good neighbor.

The player should eventually be able to say: "My coastal nursery was a very different life than my cold-region nursery."

## First Vertical Slice

Build one Godot slice that tests the heart of the game:

- Starter region.
- Small roadside stand.
- A few recurring characters.
- 12-20 plant/crop analogues.
- One weekly market board.
- One propagation bench.
- One seasonal event.
- Controller-friendly UI.
- End-of-week reflection.

The key question: is it fun and emotionally resonant to learn the place, read people, grow stock, and make recommendations?
