# Starter Region Brief: Hush Arbor

Hush Arbor is the working starter region for the first vertical slice. It should feel welcoming and readable for a new player, but not neutral. The player is learning a specific place: a mild valley town where old orchards, wet lanes, porch gardens, and practical market habits shape what people want from a nursery.

## Role In The Game

Hush Arbor teaches the core Garden Nursery promise: pay attention to living conditions and local people, then make better plant choices. It gives the player enough forgiveness to experiment while making it clear that region, season, weather, culture, and customer constraints matter.

The opening nursery should sit on the edge of a two-lane road between the town and a patchwork of small farms. It begins as a table, crates, seed trays, a handwritten market board, and a few regulars who notice what the player stocks.

## Climate And Growing Conditions

- Climate: temperate valley with gentle springs, warm but rarely punishing summers, wet autumns, and short cold snaps in winter.
- Rain pattern: reliable spring rain, occasional summer dry spells, steady autumn mist.
- Soil: deep loam in old orchard ground, clay pockets near the creek, and mossy shaded yards close to stone walls and older houses.
- Frost: late spring frost is possible but uncommon; first autumn frost is predictable enough for early tutorials.
- Wind: mild downslope evening wind dries exposed pots faster than sheltered stock.
- Constraints: drainage, shade, and timing matter more than survival extremes. Most beginner plants can succeed, but the best recommendations fit microclimates and season.

## Ecology

Hush Arbor is built around second-growth woodland, hedgerows, small orchards, creek edges, and kitchen gardens. The visual language should favor layered greens, fruit trees, damp paths, seed trays, mason jars, handwritten tags, and practical porch displays.

Common plant analogues for early systems:

- Culinary herbs: mint, chives, parsley, thyme, sage.
- Friendly vegetables: cherry tomato analogue, lettuces, beans, squash, scallions.
- Flowers: calendula, nasturtium, sweet pea, violet, yarrow.
- Perennials and shrubs: berry canes, shade fern, hardy geranium, small fruit tree starts.
- Regional specialties: mist-tolerant greens, cider-orchard herbs, porch flowers that handle partial shade.

## Seasonal Feel

- Spring: seed swaps, muddy boots, porch planters, late-frost gossip, customers asking what is safe to plant now.
- Summer: herbs, tomatoes, hanging baskets, dry-spell worry, roadside fruit traffic, more impulse buys.
- Autumn: cider press days, school garden requests, wet-weather greens, perennial divisions, fungal smells, customers preparing beds for next year.
- Winter: quiet repairs, seed catalogs, preserved herbs, evergreen cuttings, stories at the feed store, planning rather than high-volume sales.

The vertical slice can begin in spring because it gives the clearest tutorial question: what can be planted now, what should wait, and which customers understand the difference?

## Cultural And Local-Market Hooks

- Porch pride: small front gardens matter socially. Customers want plants that make homes look cared for without requiring perfect skill.
- Cider week: old orchards drive seasonal demand for pollinator flowers, herb bundles, cider spices, and small fruit starts.
- Seed-swap etiquette: locals trade cuttings and stories, but there are quiet rules about generosity, bragging, and remembering who helped you.
- Newcomer gardens: recent arrivals from denser cities want beauty and control, but many underestimate shade, clay, deer pressure, and time.
- School and cafe demand: teachers and cooks need reliable, explainable plants, creating simple recurring orders that can teach market prediction.

## Magical-Realism Hooks

- Hushmint: a mint analogue that repeats the last kind sentence it heard, but always drops one important word.
- Lantern tomatoes: small tomato analogues that glow faintly at dusk when grown with steady watering and enough afternoon warmth.
- Apology violet: a shade flower that droops around unresolved arguments and perks up after sincere repair, which locals treat as normal but embarrassing.
- Orchard weather bells: dried seed pods hung in porches click softly before mist or frost, useful but never exact.
- Borrow-root: a root vegetable that appears in neglected corners and grows better when nearby beds are cared for without being over-managed.

These should be presented matter-of-factly. Hush Arbor residents do not explain the magic as a system; they use it, joke about it, ignore it when inconvenient, and argue over what it means.

## Gentle Market-Reading Tutorial

Hush Arbor should teach market-reading through clear but imperfect signals:

- Weather board: a note about possible late frost increases demand for hardy starts and makes tender tomatoes a risk.
- Customer chatter: porch gardeners ask for color and low effort; cooks ask for herbs; teachers ask for sturdy plants with good stories.
- Browse behavior: customers linger over tomatoes before buying greens if the week feels too cold.
- Local event notices: seed swap, cider week, school garden day, and first market Saturday shape demand in visible ways.
- Plant performance: stock exposed to evening wind dries faster, while shade plants look better near the old wall.

The first lesson should not be "guess the hidden spreadsheet." It should be: listen to three clues, stock a small bet, see the result, and learn why it happened.

## Early Vertical-Slice Signals

Useful first-week signals:

- Community board: "Possible frost Thursday. Cover tender starts."
- Supplier note: "Good price on hardy greens; tomatoes are early and fussy."
- Customer line: "My porch gets morning sun and then nothing but chimney shade."
- Visual cue: the old stone-wall display keeps fern and violet starts perkier than the roadside table.

Useful first-week demand:

- Strong: hardy greens, chives, calendula, shade violets.
- Moderate: berry canes, parsley, porch flowers.
- Risky but tempting: lantern tomatoes before the weather warms.

## Tone Notes

Hush Arbor should be warm but not frictionless. People are friendly, but they have budgets, habits, pride, and wrong assumptions. The region's pressure comes from attention: if the player treats every yard and customer the same, they leave money and trust on the table.

Use specificity over lore dumps. A customer saying "the north side of my porch grows moss on a tin bucket" is more useful than a paragraph explaining regional humidity.
